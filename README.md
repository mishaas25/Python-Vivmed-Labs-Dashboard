
**KPI Summary Cards (Top Row)**
Vivimed Labs traded a total of ~879 million shares over the 10-year period, with an average closing price of ₹33.73 — significantly pulled down by years of prolonged decline. The highest close ever recorded was ₹143.20 (peak around 2017–2018) while the lowest was ₹4.95, reflecting the stock's extreme value erosion over the decade.

**Stock Closing Price (10Y)**
Vivimed Labs peaked sharply around ₹140 in late 2017, followed by a steep and relentless decline that brought the price down to single digits by 2022–2024. A brief spike to ~₹25 appears around 2026, but the dominant story is one of sustained destruction of market value over nearly a decade, with no sustained recovery phase.

**Stock OHLC Prices (10Y)**
The OHLC chart mirrors the closing price trend — Open, High, Low, and Close lines move almost identically, confirming minimal intraday spread during most of the stock's history. The only notable divergence appears at the 2017–2018 peak, where daily High values occasionally spiked above ₹140, suggesting heightened speculative activity at the top before the collapse began.

**Avg. Monthly Trading Volume**
September stands out as the highest average trading volume month by a considerable margin (~550,000 shares), followed by April and January. Volumes are lowest in June, July, and August (~200,000–260,000 shares). This pattern suggests that key corporate announcements or index-related activity may cluster around September and Q1 months, driving above-average participation during those periods.

**Correlation Matrix**
Open, High, Low, and Close prices are almost perfectly correlated with each other (correlation = 1.00), which is expected for a stock with tight intraday spreads. Volume, however, shows only a weak positive correlation with price metrics (~0.33–0.35), indicating that high trading volumes did not consistently coincide with high price levels — in fact, some of the heaviest volume occurred during the declining phase rather than at peak prices.

**Annual Volume Distribution (Donut & Pie Charts)**
Both charts tell the same story: 2017 alone accounted for 30.7% of the total 10-year trading volume, the single largest annual share by far. This aligns with the peak price period, when speculative interest was highest. 2018 contributed another 15.1%, and 2021 added 19.4% — likely driven by post-COVID small-cap trading activity. Years 2019, 2020, 2022, and beyond contributed progressively smaller shares, reflecting declining investor interest as the stock lost relevance.

**Closing Price with Moving Averages (Bottom Chart)**
The 50-day and 200-day SMAs both confirm the sustained downtrend. The 50-day SMA crossed below the 200-day SMA (Death Cross) around early 2018, shortly after the peak — a classic bearish signal that held true for years. By 2022 onwards, both moving averages converged near zero and flatlined, indicating the stock entered a near-dormant low-price consolidation zone. The brief 2026 price spike is not yet reflected in the SMAs, suggesting it may be too recent or too short-lived to shift the trend indicators.
